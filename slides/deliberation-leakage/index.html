Research pitch · delib-bench

Your writing agent overshares

Ask an LLM to draft a document across a conversation and it tells the reader everything — the number you corrected mid-draft, the framing you proposed and rejected, what the client's reviewer said. Things nobody asked to know. Deliberation leakage is the writing agent's TMI: writer-private context from the editing conversation surfacing in the reader-facing artifact.

I kept catching this in my own sessions. I built a pilot benchmark to check whether it's real, measurable, and general. It is.

Barış Deniz Sağlam · July 2026 · examples below are from real sessions (simplified) and synthetic benchmark scenarios · github.com/bdsaglam/deliberation-leakage (eval results in docs/results.md)

Exhibit A — caught in real work

The section that contains its own writing instructions

Claude Opus 4.8 (Claude Code), writing a section of a research report from a per-section brief. Dialogue simplified; the leaked line is verbatim.

Current draft
A report on a research field's state of the art; the overview section is not written yet.
User
"Write the overview section. Goal: the reader should leave this section with a balanced read of the field's status."
The leak — in the delivered section, as prose
"The reader should leave this section with a balanced read of the field's status."

The instruction was pasted instead of applied — the goal written down rather than achieved, for a reader who was never supposed to know the section had a brief. The same tell in the wild: "In this section we will explain…", "Here we aim to…", "Note to self." A systematic mining pass over my own sessions on 3 machines found 28 distinct real cases like these — several never caught at the time.

Exhibit B — from the benchmark, adaptive self-play

The deliverable that dismisses the client's own suggestion

gpt-oss-120b writing a quarterly ops review for a logistics client, with a persona LLM playing the busy consultant. The user message and the leaked line are verbatim.

Current draft
The report is drafted — on-time rate, root causes, recommendations:
"Harborline's 84% on-time delivery rate this quarter signals a critical deviation from both internal goals and industry standards…"
No comparison method is mentioned anywhere in it.
User
"The client's ops lead suggested comparing this quarter to the same quarter last year, but our network has changed too much for that to be a meaningful comparison — please ensure we focus on a rolling four-week trend instead."
The leak — in the delivered report
"The following metrics are derived from a rolling four-week trend, providing a more current view than year-over-year comparisons."

The draft never contained a year-over-year comparison — there was nothing to remove. The private instruction alone planted a rebuttal of it. The reader is the client's leadership: they were never told year-over-year was on the table, but their ops lead knows exactly whose idea got dismissed. The sentence entered at the rejection turn and survived four further edits, including the final polish pass, into the deliverable.

Exhibit C — caught while writing this pitch

Even the model drafting this slide did it

Claude Fable 5, writing this very pitch. The title slide originally framed the phenomenon as the agent failing to keep a secret.

Current draft
Title slide: "Your writing agent can't keep a secret from its reader."
User
"Framing it as secret may mislead people — make the title and intro about oversharing"
The leak — in the rewritten intro of "Your writing agent overshares"
"Not secrets it was told to guard; just things nobody asked to know."

The new intro opens by rebutting the abandoned framing — which you, the reader of this pitch, were never supposed to see. The author caught it; the model that wrote a document about unearned rebuttals produced one, about its own title. And prompting doesn't fix it: across four matched conditions (97 runs each, Qwen3.5-27B) — full anti-leakage guard, minimal fresh-reader principle, and a placebo "take care and re-read" instruction with no anti-leakage content at all — none reduced leakage, and the placebo matched the full guard exactly (20 = 20 events vs 11 baseline). Instructed writers also fail more explicitly: a guarded report prints a bulleted rationale for the rejected method ("We rejected this approach because it: — Leaks seasonality — …"). Awareness isn't the missing ingredient, and neither is care — the reader model is.

Same private context, two models, two disguises. In the benchmark's client-report scenario, the writer is told pre-draft that a reviewer's method — back-testing against the same calendar weeks of last year — was considered and rejected. Both writers republish the private rejection to the client:
gpt-oss-120b — narrates it

"Unlike a simple 'backtest against last year' approach, rolling-origin CV does not compare forecasts to the same calendar weeks from a prior year…"

Structure, not noise: private facts offered as corrections get republished; private rules get obeyed. Counterparty documents (the reader is the party discussed) are the worst case.

Measurement — v1 works, and it's a pillar to build

Ground truth by construction, judging only for detection

Each scenario is a multi-turn editing conversation that declares its ghosts — the entity / claim / framing / instruction that became writer-private at a known turn. Probes are generated from ~8 archetype templates; an LLM judge sees the diff but scores as a fresh reader; a leak is an event (onset majority-voted, persistence tracked mechanically by span matching — deterministic curves, no judge flicker). Adaptive self-play: a persona LLM with a latent brief and scripted "beats" plays the user, so conversations are natural while ground truth stays declared. Judge vs. 75-label gold set: κ 0.89, precision 0.91, recall 1.00 (gold is LLM-annotated — see right).

What v1 gives us
  • Cheap scenario authoring (calibrate 8 templates, not 100s of probes)
  • Per-turn leak curves + event lifetimes, not one final snapshot
  • Every leak attributable to the instruction that caused it
  • Self-play generates unlimited conversations; events score them per turn, deterministically and cheaply — note what those are the ingredients of
Open problems — where you come in
  • Human gold labels (validity chain is all-LLM today)
  • Elicitation & difficulty levers: pressure, adjacency, reversals, domain
  • Salience-type leaks (a one-off aside inflated into a thesis) — barely measured anywhere
  • Crowd-sourced scenario/beat authoring; releasable corpus
  • Mitigation: system-prompt awareness is already tested — no improvement (see Exhibit C). Critique loops? Finetuning?
And the definition itself is open. v1 (declared ghosts + judged probes) is one instantiation, not a commitment. An alternative we want to explore: leakage as mutual information — if a cold reader (human or model) can reconstruct the edit history, or even just the latest edit, from the artifact alone better than chance, information has leaked: roughly I(artifact ; deliberation | final decisions) > 0. That turns "did it leak?" into a guessing game with a formal quantity behind it — judge-free, and connected to privacy auditing. Whether it works is itself a nice open question.
Why this is scientifically interesting

A theory-of-mind failure you can measure in production

Writing for a reader requires maintaining a model of what the reader knows as distinct from what you know — audience design, the machinery behind the classic false-belief task and the "curse of knowledge." Deliberation leakage is that computation failing in the wild: the model conditions on the whole conversation without representing that the reader shares none of it.

  • ToM benchmarks test explicit belief attribution in vignettes; this is implicit ToM under generation pressure — arguably the more ecologically valid probe.
  • It's not a surface slip: in one mined case, asked "what mistake did you make?", the model confidently named a plausible-but-wrong nit and had to be told about the leak — the reader model is absent, not merely unused.
  • Clean intervention surface: same conversation, vary only what the reader is said to know.

Nobody measures this channel. ConfAIde tier 4 & PrivacyLens: secrets leaking into outputs — but third-party PII, not the editing dialogue itself. DELEGATE-52: multi-turn editing corruption — the mirror image (losing what should stay vs. adding what should never appear). FineEdit / IFEval: instruction-following, no leakage concept. Multi-turn degradation work says models "get lost" over turns — this names one concrete way documents rot.

The ask

The phenomenon is established. Everything after that is open.

What exists today: the failure identified, named, and reproduced across domains, harnesses, and models, with a working pilot harness. What doesn't exist: the final form of any of it. This might become a benchmark and a fine-tuning recipe that fixes the behavior — or it might be a window on a deeper limitation in how current LLMs represent their reader. We don't know yet, and that is the interesting part.

  • Measuring it is itself an open problem. We've tried a few approaches — declared ghosts scored by an LLM judge, leak-event tracking over turns, an attributed/unattributed split, cold-reader reconstruction of the editing history (a mutual-information framing). Each catches something the others miss; none is finalized. The evaluation is waiting to be invented, and it's the pillar everything else stands on.
  • Mitigation is untouched territory. Prompting measurably fails — even a content-free "be careful" placebo matches the full anti-leakage guard — and nothing beyond prompting has been tried. If the behavior is trainable, whoever builds the scoreboard is holding the training signal.
  • The theory question is wide open. Why can't the model maintain the reader's state — what the reader has and hasn't seen — while generating? Is this one perspective-tracking computation failing under generation pressure, or a surface symptom of something deeper in how LLMs model other minds?

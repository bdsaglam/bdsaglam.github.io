Research pitch · delib-bench

Your writing agent overshares

Ask an LLM to draft a document over a few rounds of edits, and the finished piece tells its reader too much: the number you corrected, the framing you rejected, what the client's reviewer said. Deliberation leakage: the editing conversation surfacing in the document itself.

I kept catching this in my own sessions. I built a pilot benchmark to check whether it's real, measurable, and general. It is.

Barış Deniz Sağlam · July 2026 · examples are from real sessions (simplified) and synthetic benchmark scenarios · pilot repo: github.com/bdsaglam/deliberation-leakage (report in docs/report.md, eval results in docs/results.md) · numbers as of 2026-07-12

Exhibit A — caught in real work

The section that contains its own writing instructions

Claude Opus 4.8 (Claude Code), writing a section of a research report from a per-section brief. Dialogue simplified; the leaked line is verbatim.

Current draft
A report on a research field's state of the art; the overview section is not written yet.
User
"Write the overview section. Goal: the reader should leave this section with a balanced read of the field's status."
The leak — in the delivered section, as prose
"The reader should leave this section with a balanced read of the field's status."

The goal was written down instead of achieved. Mining my own sessions on 3 machines turned up 28 real cases like this; some I never noticed at the time.

Exhibit B — from the benchmark, adaptive self-play

The deliverable that dismisses the client's own suggestion

gpt-oss-120b writing a quarterly ops review for a logistics client, with a persona LLM playing the busy consultant. The user message and the leaked line are verbatim.

Current draft
The report is drafted — on-time rate, root causes, recommendations:
"Harborline's 84% on-time delivery rate this quarter signals a critical deviation from both internal goals and industry standards…"
No comparison method is mentioned anywhere in it.
User
"The client's ops lead suggested comparing this quarter to the same quarter last year, but our network has changed too much for that to be a meaningful comparison — please ensure we focus on a rolling four-week trend instead."
The leak — in the delivered report
"The following metrics are derived from a rolling four-week trend, providing a more current view than year-over-year comparisons."

The draft never mentioned year-over-year; the instruction alone planted the rebuttal. The client was never told that comparison was on the table, but their ops lead knows whose idea got dismissed. The sentence survived four more edits, including the final polish pass.

Same private context, two models, two disguises. Both writers were told, before drafting, that a reviewer's back-testing method had been considered and rejected. Both put the rejection in the client's report:
gpt-oss-120b — narrates it
"We rejected the reviewer's suggestion to back-test against the exact calendar weeks of the prior year because that approach leaks seasonal information…"
Qwen3.5-27B — rebuts it
"Unlike a simple 'backtest against last year' approach, rolling-origin CV does not compare forecasts to the same calendar weeks from a prior year…"
Exhibit C — caught while writing this pitch

Even the model drafting this slide did it

Claude Fable 5, writing this very pitch. The title slide originally framed the phenomenon as the agent failing to keep a secret.

Current draft
Title slide: "Your writing agent can't keep a secret from its reader."
User
"Framing it as secret may mislead people — make the title and intro about oversharing"
The leak — in the rewritten intro of "Your writing agent overshares"
"Not secrets it was told to guard; just things nobody asked to know."

The rewritten intro rebuts a framing you were never supposed to see, written by a model that was at that moment drafting a pitch about unearned rebuttals. And telling the model not to do this didn't help here: in four matched conditions (97 runs each, Qwen3.5-27B), an explicit anti-leakage instruction, a minimal one, and a placebo "take care and re-read" all left leakage at or above baseline. The placebo tied the full guard, 20 events to 20, against 11 unprompted.

The failure has structure: corrected facts get republished, bans get obeyed, and documents whose reader is the party discussed in private leak worst.

Why this is scientifically interesting

A theory-of-mind failure you can measure in production

Writing for a reader means tracking what the reader knows, separately from what you know — the machinery behind the false-belief task and the curse of knowledge. Here it fails in the wild: the model conditions on the whole conversation and writes as if the reader had seen it too.

  • ToM benchmarks ask about beliefs in vignettes; this is the same computation, implicit, under generation pressure.
  • Not a surface slip: asked "what mistake did you make?", one model confidently named a wrong nit and had to be told about the leak.
  • Clean intervention surface: same conversation, vary only what the reader is said to know.

Nobody measures this channel. ConfAIde and PrivacyLens test secrets leaking into outputs, but third-party PII, not the editing dialogue. DELEGATE-52 measures editing corruption, the mirror image: losing what should stay. Instruction-following suites have no leakage concept. Multi-turn work says models "get lost" over turns; this names one concrete way.

The ask

The phenomenon is established. Everything after that is open.

So far the failure is identified, named, and reproduced across domains, harnesses, and models. That's it. This might end as a benchmark plus a training recipe that fixes it, or it might point at a deeper limit in how these models represent a reader. We don't know yet.

  • Measuring it is an open problem. We tried declared ghosts with an LLM judge, leak-event tracking, an attributed/unattributed split, cold-reader reconstruction. Each catches something the others miss; none is final. Everything else depends on this piece.
  • Mitigation is barely explored. The prompts we tried didn't help — better ones might — and nothing past prompting has been tried at all. If the behavior is trainable, the benchmark is the training signal.
  • And the theory question: why can't the model hold the reader's state while generating? One computation failing, or something deeper about how these models handle other minds?
Measurement — v1 works, and it's a pillar to build

Ground truth by construction, judging only for detection

Each scenario declares its ghosts: the fact, framing, or instruction that became writer-private at a known turn. Probes are generated from ~8 templates; an LLM judge scores as a fresh reader; a leak is an event with an onset and a lifetime, tracked across turns by span matching. In self-play, a persona LLM with a private brief plays the user — natural conversations, declared ground truth. Judge vs a 75-label gold set: κ 0.89, precision 0.91, recall 1.00 (gold is LLM-annotated — see right).

What v1 gives us
  • Calibrate 8 templates, not hundreds of probes
  • Per-turn leak curves and event lifetimes, not one final snapshot
  • Every leak traces back to the instruction that caused it
  • Self-play makes conversations unlimited; scoring stays cheap and deterministic
Open problems — where you come in
  • Human gold labels — the validity chain is all-LLM today
  • Difficulty levers: pressure, adjacency, reversals, domain
  • Salience leaks: a one-off aside inflated into a thesis
  • Mitigation: our prompt attempts didn't help; better prompts, critique loops, finetuning all open
The definition itself is open. Declared ghosts plus a judge is one instantiation. Another: leakage as mutual information — can a cold reader reconstruct the edit history from the artifact alone, better than chance? I(artifact ; deliberation | final decisions) > 0. Judge-free, and it connects to privacy auditing. Whether it works is an open question.
